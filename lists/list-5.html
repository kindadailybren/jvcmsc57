V. GRAPHS

Graphs in Discrete Mathematics have been one of the most fascinating topics for me. At first, they might seem like just a collection of dots (vertices) and lines (edges), but digging deeper, I realized how incredibly powerful and applicable they are in real-life scenarios. In essence, graphs are about connections — between people, places, ideas, or systems. From social media networks and transportation maps to computer networks and decision trees, graphs help model relationships and movement. The beauty of graphs lies in how something so abstract can represent something so real. One concept I particularly enjoyed was the Hamiltonian path, a path that visits each vertex exactly once. It felt like solving a puzzle: how do you plan a trip that visits every city once without repeating? This reminded me of The Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP), which is used in logistics, delivery route planning, and even DNA sequencing. There’s something oddly satisfying about finding the most efficient way to traverse a graph without redundancy. It's like being a completionist in an open-world game, trying to unlock every area in the most optimal route. Another favorite was Dijkstra's Algorithm, a method for finding the shortest path between nodes in a weighted graph. What's amazing is how often we use this algorithm without realizing it, when using Google Maps, for example. Every time I commute from UP Mindanao to anywhere in Davao, and I check Waze or Google Maps for the fastest route, Dijkstra is silently working behind the scenes. This algorithm doesn't just find a path, it finds the shortest one, considering all possible detours and travel times. That's algorithmic elegance meeting real-world usefulness.
